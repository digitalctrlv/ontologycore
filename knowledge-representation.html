4.1 The Ontology

Our ontology formalizes aesthetics as complex cultural entities that emerge through community-driven creative processes on digital platforms. By modeling their characteristic elements and behavioral patterns, we can better understand how contemporary aesthetics differ from traditional subcultures in their formation and dissemination, and how they offer new popular ways of being creative through the reuse and recontextualization of past creative products.

Aesthetics are abstract, dynamic, and modern concepts. With our ontology, we aim to map their characteristic elements, relevant behaviors, and the creative processes involved in their creation and dissemination. The previous chapters introduced three theoretical frameworks that helped us create these focus points and position these intangible cultural commodities within creative theory.

There have been numerous resources classifying what belongs to an aesthetic and what does not. For example, we made deliberate use of the independent, crowd-sourced Aesthetics Wiki for identifying features of our selected aesthetics. Essentially, this classification task represents the genesis of aesthetics themselves. Aesthetics inherit from their 'parent class' of Subcultures the practice of inclusion and exclusion. This practice operates through various cultural forms such as art, music, and clothing, often based on ethnicity, interests, and social background.

Undeniably, these types of social groupings are extremely similar. Yet recent aesthetics such as Clean Girl, Y2K, and Dark Academia demonstrate distinctive characteristics through Metaconvergence. This term is coined in the earlier mentioned CreOn ontology and has become an increasingly popular method of expressing creativity on digital platforms. To clarify this concept within our framework, we interpret the term in our ontology core as follows:

"Metaconvergence is the process by which individuals or groups combine elements from multiple subcultures or aesthetics to create new, hybrid forms of expression. This phenomenon is particularly prevalent in digital spaces, where the rapid exchange of ideas and cultural artifacts facilitates the blending of diverse influences. Metaconvergence allows for greater creativity and innovation, as individuals draw upon a wider range of sources to inform their aesthetic choices. It also reflects the fluid and dynamic nature of contemporary culture, where traditional boundaries between subcultures are increasingly blurred."

Bricolage, a creative technique within our CreativeProcess class, appears similar to the Montage and Collage art techniques that gained significant popularity during the Dada art movement in the first half of the twentieth century. Reusing existing visual elements is not an uncommon phenomenon in art and creativity. However, contemporary aesthetics like Y2K operate differently. Y2K's reproduction of a 2000s 'aesthetic' evokes particular emotions such as nostalgia or a sense of belonging to a period that users may have never even experienced. Creative products from the past are recontextualized at rapid speed, not just by one dominant artist but by an anonymous web community. Often, the motive is not protest against dominant culture but rather a means to gain acknowledgment for stylistic efforts and to shape identity.

From here, with our ontology, we aim not only bring aesthetics into existence but also formalize the specific creative processes present in their emergence. It is valuable to move beyond understanding aesthetics as predominantly formalistic subcultures. Instead, we can observe how they facilitate a modern, grassroots, community-driven approach to identity formation and art making.

We initially selected six aesthetics and adapted our model to their characteristics: Y2K, Cottagecore, Dark Academia, Grunge, Old Money and Clean Girl. Our selection aimed to cover a variety of aesthetics that address different temporal contexts and demonstrate distinct creative phases. Notwithstanding, we recognize that there is considerable overlap since their usage and emergence occur around the same time period. Therefore, our analysis will show that most of them use the same types of media channels to disseminate the aesthetic. This shared infrastructure highlights the interconnected nature of contemporary aesthetic movements and their collective reliance on digital platforms for expression and growth.

4.2 Modeling

In this section we will describe how we used LLMs to model our ontology using a bottom-up extreme design methodolgy and CLOWN. During this phase, we systematically generated definitions, descriptions, and user scenarios for each aesthetic. This generated content informed the development of competency questions, which provided the structural foundation for constructing the ontology.

    Property Definitions:
    characterizedBy
    Links aesthetics to their defining visual and sensory elements, establishing the core visual vocabulary of each aesthetic movement.
    involvesProcess
    Connects aesthetics or processes with other processes essential for the aesthetic's development and creation, mapping the creative workflow.
    involvesEmotion / involvesBehaviour
    Establishes connections between aesthetics and specific social/emotional profiles of both creators and consumers, defining the psychological dimensions of aesthetic engagement.
    hasCulturalContext / hasSpatialContext / hasTemporalContext
    Links aesthetics or objects to their contextual environments (culture/space/time), documenting the conditions under which specific individuals were created or emerged.
    experiencedBy
    Specifies which demographic or social groups actively practice and engage with the aesthetic, mapping audience participation.
    hasMedium
    Defines the channels and platforms through which creative products are expressed and distributed, mapping dissemination pathways.
    spreadThrough
    Identifies the media platforms and channels responsible for propagating and popularizing aesthetic movements across digital ecosystems.
    resultsIn
    Documents the creative outputs and tangible products generated through aesthetic processes, connecting creative activities to their material outcomes.
    inspiredBy
    Traces genealogical influences and inspirational sources between aesthetic movements, mapping the evolution and cross-pollination of visual styles.
    influencedBy
    Records the impact of specific influencers, creators, or media entities on the development and popularization of aesthetic movements.
    createdBy
    Attributes the origin and authorship of aesthetics to specific communities, individuals, or collective creative forces.
    opposesTo
    Maps ideological and stylistic oppositions between aesthetic movements, highlighting contrasting values and visual approaches.
    precedesProcess
    Defines temporal sequences in creative workflows, establishing the order of process phases from initial ideation to final refinement.
    revivedIn / revivesElement
    Documents temporal recycling and nostalgic revivals, tracking how historical aesthetic elements are reintroduced in contemporary contexts.
    expressesAesthetic
    Indicates how specific elements, products, or media directly manifest and represent particular aesthetic qualities and principles.
    uses
    Specifies the tools, technologies, and resources employed in the creation and production of aesthetic content and products.
    shapesAesthetic
    Describes how contextual factors, conceptual ideas, or cultural movements actively form and influence the development of aesthetic characteristics.
